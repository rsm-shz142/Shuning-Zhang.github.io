Introduction

Does raising the minimum wage cause unemployment? Standard economics textbooks say yes: if you force employers to pay workers more, they will simply hire fewer of them. Card and Krueger (1994) challenged this prediction head-on using a remarkably clean natural experiment.

On April 1, 1992, New Jersey raised its minimum wage from $4.25 to $5.05 per hour — the highest state minimum wage in the country at the time. Pennsylvania, right next door, kept its minimum wage unchanged at $4.25. Card and Krueger surveyed 410 fast-food restaurants (Burger King, KFC, Roy Rogers, and Wendy’s) in both states before the increase (February–March 1992) and after (November–December 1992).

This setup is the classic Difference-in-Differences (DiD) design:

  • Treatment group: New Jersey restaurants (subject to the wage increase)
  • Control group: Pennsylvania restaurants (no change)
  • Key assumption: Without the minimum wage increase, employment trends in both states would have moved in parallel

The DiD estimator subtracts two differences:

\[ \hat{\tau}_{DiD} = (\bar{Y}_{NJ,after} - \bar{Y}_{NJ,before}) - (\bar{Y}_{PA,after} - \bar{Y}_{PA,before}) \]

By comparing the change in NJ to the change in PA, we difference out any seasonal trends or macroeconomic shocks that affected both states equally — leaving only the effect of the minimum wage.

Their headline finding: employment in New Jersey did not fall after the minimum wage increase. In fact, it rose slightly relative to Pennsylvania, directly contradicting the standard competitive model.

How to read this table:

  • Before the wage increase, NJ stores employed slightly fewer workers than PA stores (20.44 vs. 23.33).
  • After the increase, NJ employment rose while PA employment fell.
  • The DiD estimate of +2.76 FTE employees (our replication: +2.75) is positive and statistically significant (t ≈ 2.03), directly contradicting the prediction that raising the minimum wage reduces employment.

Sample size:        351  (paper: 357)

The coefficient on the NJ dummy (2.28, paper: 2.33) tells us that, controlling for nothing else, NJ stores gained about 2.3 more FTE employees than PA stores between Wave 1 and Wave 2. The standard error of 1.19 matches the paper exactly. The small discrepancy in the point estimate likely reflects minor differences in handling temporarily closed stores.

Something Additional: Visualizing the Wage Distribution Shift

One of the most compelling pieces of evidence in the paper is Figure 1: the distribution of starting wages in NJ and PA before and after the minimum wage increase. The paper only shows this as a histogram — here we recreate and extend it to make the Diff-in-Diff logic visually intuitive.

The key insight: if the minimum wage is truly binding (i.e., it forced real wage changes), we should see NJ’s wage distribution shift dramatically toward $5.05 after April 1992, while PA’s distribution stays flat. This is exactly what happened — and it’s the “first stage” that validates the whole study.

Share of stores paying exactly $5.05 (new minimum):
  NJ Wave 2: 89.0%  (paper: 85.2%)
  PA Wave 2: 1.4%  (paper:  1.3%)

Share of stores still paying $4.25 (old minimum):
  NJ Wave 1: 32.2%  (paper: 30.5%)
  PA Wave 1: 34.2%  (paper: 32.9%)
  NJ Wave 2: 0.0%  (paper:  0.0%)
  PA Wave 2: 28.2%  (paper: 25.3%)

What this shows:

  • Before the increase (Wave 1), NJ and PA had nearly identical wage distributions — about 30% of stores in both states paid exactly the old minimum of $4.25/hr. This similarity supports the parallel-trends assumption underlying the DiD design.

  • After the increase (Wave 2), 85% of NJ stores piled up right at the new $5.05 floor, while Pennsylvania’s distribution barely moved. By Wave 2, zero NJ stores still paid $4.25.

  • This dramatic shift is the “first stage” of the causal chain: the law actually changed wages, so any subsequent employment effects can be attributed to the policy rather than coincidence.

  • Despite this large wage shock, NJ employment increased relative to PA — the central paradox that motivated the paper and challenged a generation of economists to rethink the competitive model of the labor market.
