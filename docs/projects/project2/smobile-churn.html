S-Mobile: Predicting Customer Churn

Built a churn prediction and retention strategy framework for a telecom with 1M subscribers — identifying key drivers and quantifying up to $258 CLV uplift per customer from targeted interventions.

One-line summary: Built an end-to-end churn analytics pipeline for Singapore’s S-Mobile — from predictive modeling and driver analysis to actionable retention strategies with full 5-year CLV evaluation, identifying up to $258 incremental CLV per customer from the most effective intervention.

Business Problem

S-Mobile, a leading Singapore telecom with approximately 1 million subscribers, faced a 2% monthly churn rate. The traditional response — waiting for customers to call in and threaten to leave — was reactive and trained customers to game the system for discounts.

The goal was to build a proactive churn management system: predict which customers are likely to leave before they do, identify the root causes, design targeted interventions, and evaluate the economics of each action.

Methodology

Data & Setup

  • 39,000 training observations + 30,000 representative sample (2% real-world churn rate)
  • Features: device characteristics, usage behavior (minutes, overage, dropped calls), customer demographics (occupation, credit rating, tenure), service history
  • Key modeling challenge: training data was balanced at 50% churn for model fitting, then predictions were probability-corrected back to the true 2% population rate for downstream CLV analysis

Step 1 — Churn Prediction Model

Built and compared two models:

Model Validation AUC
Logistic Regression (final) ~0.70
Random Forest (tuned, RandomizedSearchCV) ~0.695

Random Forest did not materially outperform Logistic Regression on the validation set, suggesting the data has limited nonlinear structure. Logistic Regression was selected as the final model for its interpretability and equivalent predictive performance. Variable car was dropped due to consistent statistical insignificance.

Step 2 — Key Churn Drivers

Using Permutation Importance and Partial Dependence Plots, the top 5 churn drivers identified were:

Driver Finding
Occupation Students churn at far higher rates than retired customers
Equipment age (eqpdays) Older devices strongly predict switching behavior
Overage usage High overage minutes signal plan mismatch and billing frustration
Credit rating Low-credit customers are significantly less stable
Tenure (months) Longer-tenure customers are much less likely to churn

Step 3 — Retention Actions

Five targeted interventions were designed based on the churn drivers, each applied only to high-risk customers in the relevant segment:

  • Early device upgrade program — for high-risk customers with handsets above 75th percentile age
  • Plan optimization — for high-risk customers with consistently high overage usage
  • Student retention offer — flexible plans for students with above-median churn probability
  • Credit auto-pay incentive — for low-credit customers with high predicted churn risk
  • Service quality improvement — for high-risk customers experiencing frequent dropped calls

Step 4 — Quantifying Churn Reduction

Using model-based counterfactual predictions on the representative sample (baseline churn rate = 1.99%):

Intervention Churn Rate Reduction
Credit auto-pay enrollment −0.88 pp
Early device upgrade −0.35 pp
Plan optimization −0.31 pp
Student retention offer −0.04 pp
Service quality improvement −0.02 pp

Key Results

CLV Economics (5-Year, 10% Annual Discount Rate)

Baseline assumptions: $40/month revenue per customer, 2% monthly churn, 60-month window.

Intervention Churn Reduction Incremental CLV (Breakeven) Proposed Cost
Credit Auto-pay −0.88 pp +$258/customer $40–$60
Early Upgrade −0.35 pp +$93/customer $50–$70
Plan Optimization −0.31 pp +$82/customer $30–$50

Baseline 5-year CLV per customer: $1,156

All three interventions generate positive economic returns under realistic cost assumptions. Credit auto-pay delivers the highest leverage — a $258 CLV buffer against a $40–$60 incentive cost.

Business Insights

1. Reactive churn management is inferior. The model-based proactive approach targets customers before they decide to leave, when retention offers are more effective and less expensive.

2. Not all interventions are equal. Credit auto-pay delivers 3× the CLV impact of plan optimization. Prioritizing interventions by economic return — not just churn reduction — is critical for budget efficiency.

3. Probability correction matters for economics. Predictions from a balanced training set (50% churn) would wildly overstate actual churn risk. Correcting back to the 2% population rate is essential before any CLV or cost-benefit calculation.

4. Targeting beats mass deployment. Applying interventions only to high-risk customers within the relevant driver segment maximizes ROI. For example, offering upgrade incentives to all customers wastes budget on low-risk customers who would have stayed anyway.

5. Model validation should use RCT. The counterfactual estimates provide a sound starting point, but a pilot randomized experiment is needed before large-scale rollout to confirm the real-world churn reductions are statistically significant.
