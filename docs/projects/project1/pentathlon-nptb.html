Pentathlon: Next Product to Buy Modeling

Built an NPTB framework with 4 ML models to personalize promotional emails for 5M customers — increasing profit by 22% over random assignment.

One-line summary: Built a Next Product to Buy (NPTB) framework using four machine learning models to determine the optimal promotional email for each of 5 million customers — personalizing messaging increased expected profit by 22% over random assignment and 9% over the best single-message strategy.

Business Problem

Pentathlon, a multi-department e-commerce sports retailer, was sending promotional emails without a systematic way to decide which department’s message to send each customer. The choices were: Endurance, Strength, Water, Team, Backcountry, Racquet, or no message at all.

A prior RCT revealed a key tension: higher email frequency boosted short-term revenue but significantly increased unsubscribe rates, reducing long-term customer lifetime value. The goal of this project was to move from a one-size-fits-all approach to customer-level personalized messaging that maximizes expected profit.

Methodology

Data & Setup

  • 600,000 customer–email observations (70/30 train/test split)
  • Features: demographics (age, gender, income, education, children) + historical purchase frequency across 6 departments
  • Target: whether the customer made a purchase within 2 days of receiving the email (buyer), and if so, the order size (total_os)

NPTB Framework

For each customer, we estimated the expected profit of sending each of the 7 possible messages:

\[\text{Expected Profit} = P(\text{purchase} \mid \text{message}) \times \hat{\text{order size}} \times 0.4\]

The 0.4 margin reflects a 60% COGS assumption. Each customer was then assigned the message yielding the highest expected profit.

Models Built

We built and compared four model types for predicting purchase probability:

Model Approach Test AUC
Logistic Regression (interaction) Message × customer feature interactions ≈ 0.88
Neural Network (MLP) Tuned on hidden layer size, alpha ≈ 0.88
Random Forest Tuned on depth, n_estimators ≈ 0.88
XGBoost Tuned on learning rate, depth, subsample ≈ 0.88

Key modeling insight: A baseline logistic model (without interaction terms) assigned 100% of customers to the same “endurance” message — no personalization at all. Adding message × feature interactions diversified recommendations across multiple departments, enabling true NPTB modeling.

A separate OLS regression on buyers only estimated conditional order size per message, which was combined with purchase probability to compute expected profit.

Key Results

Personalization Substantially Outperforms Every Alternative

Strategy Avg. Expected Profit / Customer Total Profit (5M customers)
Personalized (XGBoost) €0.684 €3.42M
Best Single Message (Endurance) €0.628 €3.14M
Random Assignment €0.561 €2.81M
No Message (Control) €0.431 €2.15M
  • Personalization vs. random assignment: +€616K (+22%)
  • Personalization vs. best single message: +€283K (+9%)
  • Personalization vs. no message: +€1.27M (+59%)

Message Distribution Shifts When Profit Is the Goal

When optimizing for purchase probability, ~70% of customers were assigned “Endurance” — one message dominated.

When optimizing for expected profit (purchase probability × order size × margin), the distribution became more balanced:

  • Endurance: ~43%
  • Backcountry: ~36%
  • Strength: ~17%

This shift reveals that Backcountry messages, while not the most likely to generate a purchase, tend to generate much larger orders — making them more profitable for a significant segment of customers.

Why XGBoost Was Selected as the Final Model

All four models achieved similar test AUC (~0.88), so AUC alone did not differentiate them. We selected the final model based on personalized expected profit per customer on the test set — XGBoost produced the highest value across all strategies.

Business Insights

1. Personalization generates real money at scale. A ~9% improvement over the best uniform strategy translates to nearly €300K in additional profit per 5M-customer campaign — a figure that compounds across multiple campaigns per year.

2. Order size matters, not just purchase likelihood. Optimizing on probability alone misses high-value customers who respond to niche categories with large basket sizes (e.g., backcountry gear). The NPTB framework captures this.

3. For some customers, no email is optimal. Including the no-message option in the decision framework prevents over-messaging customers who are unlikely to convert regardless of the department — protecting long-term engagement and LTV.

4. The proposed 50/50 email policy has room to improve. Pentathlon’s proposed new policy (splitting emails equally between the top 2 departments per customer) improves over random, but does not maximize profit. A fully individualized allocation would outperform the fixed split, especially when the gap between the first and second best message is large.
